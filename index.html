Chemical Equations and Balancing

Essential Study Guide for Your Chemistry Test

Overall Summary

Chemical equations are the symbolic representation of chemical reactions. The most fundamental requirement for any chemical equation is that it must be balanced. This act of balancing is required to uphold the Law of Conservation of Mass, which dictates that atoms cannot be created or destroyed during a chemical reaction. Therefore, the total number of atoms for each element must be equal on both the reactant and product sides. Mastery requires identifying the components of an equation, interpreting formulas correctly (including polyatomic ions), and applying balancing coefficients systematically.

Key Concepts

  • The Law of Conservation of Mass: This law is the primary reason we balance equations. It ensures that the mass of the reactants equals the mass of the products.
  • Coefficients vs. Subscripts:
    • Coefficients are large whole numbers placed in front of a formula to balance the number of atoms. Changing a coefficient changes the amount of the substance.
    • Subscripts are small numbers within a formula and cannot be changed. Changing a subscript changes the identity of the substance.
  • Combustion Products: A typical combustion reaction (a hydrocarbon reacting with O2) always produces the same two products: CO2 (carbon dioxide) and H2O (water).
  • Balancing Strategy (Combustion): When balancing combustion, the most effective order is generally to balance Carbon (C) first, then Hydrogen (H), and finally Oxygen (O) last.
  • Polyatomic Ion Rule: If a polyatomic ion (like NO3- or SO42-) remains intact (unchanged) on both sides of the equation, treat it as a single unit when counting atoms for balancing.
  • Diatomic Elements: Remember the 'Magnificent Seven' elements that exist as diatomic molecules when uncombined: H2, N2, O2, F2, Cl2, Br2, and I2.

Vocabulary List

Reactants:
The starting materials, written on the left side of the arrow.
Products:
The substances formed by the reaction, written on the right side of the arrow.
Coefficients:
The numbers used to balance an equation; they represent the molar ratio of substances.
Subscript:
The small number in a chemical formula indicating the number of atoms of an element in a molecule.
Aqueous:
A state symbol meaning the substance is dissolved in water.
Catalyst:
A substance that speeds up a reaction without being consumed; often written above the arrow.
Reversible Reaction:
A symbol indicating the reaction can proceed in both the forward and reverse directions.

Key Questions for Review (Active Recall)

  1. What is the final, balanced form of the methane combustion reaction: CH4 + ... O2 → ... CO2 + ... H2O?
  2. If you have 4Ca3(PO4)2, how many total oxygen atoms are present?
  3. Explain why you cannot change the subscript in H2O to H2O2 while balancing an equation.
  4. What are the four common state symbols used in chemical equations, and what does each one indicate?
  5. How would you balance a reaction involving a polyatomic ion like SO42-?
